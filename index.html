Early Warning Systems for Saibai Island

Enhancing Disaster Resilience in the Torres Strait

Executive Summary

Saibai Island, located in the Torres Strait, faces existential threats from climate change, including rising sea levels, frequent flooding, storm surges, and king tides that exceed the island's 2.3-meter seawall. With an average elevation of just one meter above sea level and a population of approximately 340 residents, the community is highly vulnerable to these hazards, which exacerbate erosion, saltwater intrusion, cultural displacement, and disruptions to daily life. Current early warning systems rely on manual processes prone to delays and human error, compounded by unreliable internet and limited infrastructure.

Our proposed solution is a hybrid Community News System: a mobile app integrated with radio wave backups, designed to deliver timely, automated alerts while respecting cultural protocols and ensuring accessibility for all residents, including elders. This system aligns with the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction, emphasizing risk knowledge, monitoring, dissemination, and preparedness. It incorporates redundancy through multiple channels (app notifications, radio broadcasts, and sirens), offline functionality, and low-power IoT for sustainability.

The design meets key criteria: redundancy, ample warning time, cultural appropriateness, reachability, coherence, and reliability. Implementation is phased over 12-18 months, starting in 2026, with an initial cost of $44,000-$70,000, funded through grants from Engineers Without Borders (EWB), Torres Strait Island Regional Council (TSIRC), and NGOs. Long-term maintenance promotes community independence via training and local management.

This solution empowers the Saibai community to adapt to climate risks, preserving cultural heritage and enhancing resilience. Broader recommendations include ongoing climate monitoring and integration with regional adaptation plans.

Background

Saibai Island, part of the Torres Strait Islands in Queensland, Australia, is a low-lying coral cay with a population of around 340 residents, primarily Torres Strait Islanders of the Koeybuway and Moegibuway peoples (Indigenous.gov.au, 2021). Situated just 4 km from Papua New Guinea, the island's remote location and average elevation of one meter above sea level make it exceptionally vulnerable to climate change impacts (Engineers Without Borders, 2025a).

Climate projections indicate sea levels in the Torres Strait could rise by 0.5-1 meter by 2100, exacerbating risks from flooding, storm surges, and king tides peaking at 4 meters (Green et al., 2009). Historical events, such as the 2006 king tide that inundated homes and cemeteries, highlight the urgency (Wellauer et al., 2025). These hazards not only threaten physical infrastructure but also cultural sites, food security, and habitability, leading to fears of forced relocation (Babacan et al., 2021).

Current practices involve the Community Disaster Management Group (CDMG) and Local Disaster Coordination Centre (LDCC), which rely on email alerts and manual dissemination (Torres Strait Island Regional Council, 2025). However, unreliable telecommunications—limited to 3G/4G with frequent outages—and human dependencies hinder effectiveness (TSRA, 2018). The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction, 2015) provides a global blueprint, emphasizing four elements: risk knowledge, monitoring, dissemination, and preparedness. Our project builds on this to reinforce ICT infrastructure, improving daily livelihoods and disaster response.

To better protect and prepare the residents of Saibai Island, this project addresses the remote location and small population by integrating with existing ICT elements like databases, cloud services, and processing power. This not only improves network coverage and speed but also enhances overall community resilience against increasing climate hazards.

Problem Description

Residents of Saibai Island require timely, reliable early warnings due to the island's proneness to severe weather events amplified by climate change. The island's highest point is only 1.7 meters, leaving it at risk from:

  • Flooding and Erosion: Damages seawalls and roads, weakening infrastructure and disrupting access (Torres Strait Island Regional Council, 2025).
  • Saltwater Intrusion: Contaminates soil and groundwater, harming gardens and freshwater supplies (Wellauer et al., 2025).
  • Cultural Identity Threats: Erosion erodes cemeteries and ancestral lands, risking cultural displacement (Tearfund Australia, n.d.).
  • Habitability Challenges: Rising tides make activities like night fishing dangerous and daily life unreliable (Voices of hard-to-reach island communities, 2025).

The existing system involves manual assessment by LDCC, email warnings, and CDMG dissemination, but issues include:

  1. Lack of automation, leading to delays.
  2. Diverse hazards (e.g., floods, bushfires) not fully accounted for.
  3. Internet inaccessibility for some residents.
  4. Human error and slow response times.

Without improvement, these gaps could result in loss of life, property, and cultural heritage, as seen in past inundations (Traditional community-based knowledge, 2025).

"How might we design an early warning system for Saibai Island that is automated, culturally appropriate, and able to keep residents safe during a diverse range of disasters?"

Project Scope

The project focuses on developing an ICT-based early warning system (EWS) to address disaster preparedness for Saibai Island's residents. User needs: Timely alerts to prepare for hazards, clear procedures, minimal human error, and independent component functionality.

Key objectives:

  • Provide advance information for preparation.
  • Ensure awareness of response protocols.
  • Automate processes to reduce errors.
  • Design modular components for resilience.

Inclusions: App development, radio integration, community training.

Exclusions: Physical infrastructure like seawalls; focuses solely on ICT alerts.

This aligns with TSIRC's adaptation plans and EWB's design brief for sustainable, community-centered solutions (Engineers Without Borders, 2025b).

Design Requirements

Based on client and community needs, the following criteria were established:

Criterion Description
Redundancy Multiple alert layers (e.g., app, radio, sirens) to ensure functionality if one fails.
Ample Time Provide sufficient warning (e.g., hours ahead) for evacuation or preparation.
Sustainability & Cultural Appropriateness Low-resource use; incorporate local languages, trusted voices, and respect traditions (e.g., no disruption to cultural sites).
Reachability Cover all 340 residents, regardless of location or tech access.
Coherence Clear, actionable warnings in simple language.
Reliability & Accuracy Function in emergencies; minimize false alerts through precise monitoring.

These link to TSIRC priorities for water security, cultural preservation, and resilience (Torres Strait Regional Adaptation Plan, n.d.).

Design Options and Evaluation

Options considered:

  1. Alarm/Siren System: Loud, immediate alerts but lacks detail and pre-warning.
  2. Satellite System: Reliable in remote areas but high cost and complex.
  3. Mobile Alert System: App-based, customizable but internet-dependent.
  4. News System: Broadcast-style but limited automation.

Evaluation against criteria (scored 1-5):

Option Redundancy Ample Time Sustainability Reachability Coherence Reliability Total
Siren 3 2 4 5 2 4 20
Satellite 4 4 2 4 3 5 22
Mobile 4 5 3 3 5 4 24
News (Hybrid) 5 5 5 5 5 5 30

Drawbacks of alternatives: News systems risk delays without backups; sirens lack nuance.

Chosen: Hybrid News System for best fit.

(Word count: 300)

Chosen Design Solution

The final solution is a Community News System: A hybrid mobile app with radio wave backup.

  • App Features: Real-time alerts, evacuation maps, offline cached plans, customizable settings (e.g., language: English, Kalaw Lagaw Ya).
  • Radio Integration: VHF/FM broadcasts for offline alerts via IoT gateway.
  • Automation: Sensors detect hazards; alerts disseminated automatically.

Justification: Meets all criteria, addresses current gaps (e.g., no redundancy), and is feasible with low costs. It outperforms alternatives by combining digital and analog channels, ensuring 100% reach.

This aligns with Sendai Framework elements and community feedback for culturally sensitive tech (Brady, 2025).

Technical Specifications

  • Backend: Firebase (Google Cloud) for database, authentication, hosting, cloud messaging.
  • Radio: IoT gateway for VHF/FM decoding.
  • Offline: Cached alerts, local storage.
  • Performance: Supports 4,000 users; <2s alert speed.
  • Security: HTTPS, data encryption.
  • Accessibility: High-contrast colors, sound alerts, multilingual.
